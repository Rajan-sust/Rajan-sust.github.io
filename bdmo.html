Problem 01:
Find all possible non-negative integer solution \((x,y)\) of the following equation: $$ x! + 2^y = (x+1)! $$ (Secondary; 2023)
Solution 01:
We know, \begin{align*} n! &= n \times (n-1) \times \dots \times 3 \times 2 \times 1\\ &= n \times (n-1)! \end{align*} Given that, \begin{align*} x! + 2^y &= (x+1)! \\ \implies 2^y &= (x+1)! - x! \\ \implies 2^y &= (x+1) \times x! - x! \\ \implies 2^y &= x! \times \{ (x+1) - 1 \} \\ \implies 2^y &= x! \times x \tag{1} \\ \end{align*}
From equation \(1\), it is clear that \(x \ngtr 2 \). When \(x\) is greater than \(2\), such as \(x = 5\), prime numbers \(3, 5\) emerge that cannot be represented as a power of \(2\).
Therefore, \((x, y) = \{(1, 0), (2, 2)\} \)
Problem 02:
For any positive integer \(n\), \(f(n)\) to be the smallest positive integer that does not divide \(n\). For example, \(f(1) = 2, f(6) = 4\). Prove that for any positive integer \(n\), either \(f(f(n))\) or \(f(f(f(n)))\) must be equal to \(2\).
(Higher Secondary; 2023)
Solution 02:
$$ n = \begin{cases} 2k - 1 & \\ 2k & \end{cases} ; k \in \mathbb{N} $$ If \(n = 2k - 1\), \begin{align*} f(f(f(2k - 1))) &= f(f(2)) \\ &= f(3) \\ &= 2 \end{align*} If \(n = 2k \), then according to Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic [1]: $$ 2k = {P_1}^{n_1} \times {P_2}^{n_2} \times \dots \times {P_m}^{n_m} $$ where \(P_1, P_2, P_3, \dots \) denotes prime numbers \(2, 3, 5, \dots\) respectively and \(n_i\) denotes non-negative integer but \(n_m\) positive integer \begin{align*} f(f(2k)) &= f(f({P_1}^{n_1} \times {P_2}^{n_2} \times \dots \times {P_m}^{n_m})) \\ &= f(min({P_1}^{n_1 + 1}, {P_2}^{n_2 + 1}, \dots, {P_m}^{n_m + 1}, {P_{m+1}}^{1})) \\ &= f(P^x)\\ &= 2 \end{align*} Reference:
[1] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic
Problem 03:
Find all solutions for real \(x\): $$ {\left\lfloor x \right\rfloor}^{3} - 7 {\left\lfloor x + \frac{1}{3} \right\rfloor} = -13 $$
Secondary; 2022
Solution 03:
Let,
\(\left\lfloor x \right\rfloor = a, \left\lfloor x + \frac{1}{3} \right\rfloor = b; a, b \in \mathbb{Z} \)
Given that,
\begin{align*} {\left\lfloor x \right\rfloor}^{3} - 7 {\left\lfloor x + \frac{1}{3} \right\rfloor} &= -13 \\ \implies a^3 - 7b &= -13 \\ \implies a^3 + 13 &= 7b \\ \implies 7b &= a^3 + 13 \\ \therefore b &= \frac{a^3 + 13}{7} \end{align*}
\[ \begin{array}{c|c|c|c} \hline \text{a} & {{a}^{3} + 13} & \text{b} & \text{(a,b)} \\ \hline 1 & 1^3 + 13 = 14 & 2 & (1, 2) \\ 2 & 2^3 + 13 = 21 & 3 & (2, 3) \\ 3 & 3^3 + 13 = 40 & 7 \nmid 40 & \\ 4 & 4^3 + 13 = 77 & 11 & (4, 11) \\ -1 & (-1)^3 + 13 = 12 & 7 \nmid 12 & \\ -2 & (-2)^3 + 13 = 5 & 7 \nmid 5 & \\ -3 & (-3)^3 + 13 = - 14 & -2 & (-3, -2) \\ \hline \end{array} \]
We know, \( \left\lfloor x \right\rfloor = n; n \in \mathbb{Z} \implies n \le x < n + 1 \)
If \((a, b) = (1, 2)\),
\begin{align*} a &= 1 \\ \implies \left\lfloor x \right\rfloor &= 1 \\ \implies 1 \le x < 2 \\ \therefore x = [1, 2) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} b &= 2 \\ \implies \left\lfloor x + \frac{1}{3} \right\rfloor &= 2 \\ \implies 2 \le (x + \frac{1}{3}) < 3 \\ \implies 2 - \frac{1}{3} \le x < 3 - \frac{1}{3} \\ \implies \frac{5}{3} \le x < \frac{8}{3} \\ \therefore x = [\frac{5}{3}, \frac{8}{3}) \end{align*}
The common region is = \( [1, 2) \cap [\frac{5}{3}, \frac{8}{3}) = [\frac{5}{3}, 2) \)
Similarly, the common region for \( (2, 3), (-3, -2)\) is \([\frac{8}{3}, 3), [\frac{-7}{3}, -2) \) respectively
If \(|a-b| > 1\), then the common region is \( \emptyset \), as a result others \((a, b)\) are ignored. The final answer is:
\( [\frac{5}{3}, 2) \cup [\frac{8}{3}, 3) \cup [\frac{-7}{3}, -2) \)
